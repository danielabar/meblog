The concern behind live coding assessments is understandable: how do you know someone can actually do the work? It's a legitimate question — many hiring managers have been burned by candidates who interviewed well but couldn't deliver. But a one-hour coding test is a narrow answer to it. It measures recall under pressure, not whether someone can navigate a large codebase, figure out *what* to build, or ship software that solves real problems. A stronger signal is a verifiable record: public code, projects taken from idea to working product, technical writing that demonstrates the reasoning, and continuous learning over years.

I've been a professional software engineer for over fifteen years, and I've been sharing my work publicly for the last 10+ years — through GitHub projects, technical blog posts, and presentations. What follows includes recent professional work and public projects going back many years. In many cases the two feed each other — lessons from production work become blog posts and demo repos.

If you're deciding whether to continue a conversation with me, this is a record you can review at your own pace — the same hour you'd spend on a live coding session, with considerably more to show for it.

## Professional Work

I'm currently a Senior Rails Developer at an EHR (Electronic Health Records) SaaS focused on nutrition and meal planning for health care practitioners. Here's a sample of projects I've delivered:

**Global Search Refresh:** The platform's existing search used SQL ILIKE pattern matching, and displayed results in a small modal. It couldn't search recipes by ingredients, couldn't rank by relevance, and couldn't unify results across content types. I replaced it with PostgreSQL full-text search across five content types, a typeahead input in the top navigation showing grouped results, and a new search results page following our new design system. A key architectural decision was introducing server-rendered Rails with Stimulus alongside the existing Backbone/Marionette SPA — letting the team build new features in modern Rails without the cost or risk of a full frontend rewrite.

The project was deliberately phased: search results shipped first to deliver value quickly, then a tag-based filter sidebar with 90+ tags across seven categories was added as a second delivery once the foundation was proven. That incremental approach — feature-flagged, shipped across multiple PRs — reduced risk while keeping the product moving forward and delivering value to our customers. The search engine required custom SQL rather than the pg_search gem's built-in query generation to handle user-scoped filtering, subscription tier gating, and the different matching strategies needed for content search versus tag filtering.

After the initial delivery, the same technology was extended to the meal planner's recipe search — a persistent pain point where practitioners couldn't find recipes unless they typed the exact beginning of a title. After the switch, practitioners reported they could actually find what they needed. One of the performance techniques I developed during this work became a [blog post](https://danielabaron.me/blog/speed-up-pg-fts-with-persistent-ts-vectors/) and a [companion demo repo](https://github.com/danielabar/recipe_search_demo).

**Platform maintenance and reliability:** I led zero-downtime upgrades from Ruby 2.7 to 3.2, Rails 6.1 to 7.2, and Heroku stack 20 to 22. I also integrated Datadog APM from scratch and [published a guide](https://danielabaron.me/blog/datadog-heroku-rails/) since no comprehensive one existed at the time.

That APM investment paid off immediately: the Ruby upgrade quietly broke Microsoft Edge support for all users. A gem dependency update changed how User-Agent strings were parsed, and an eight-year-old browser detection rule started blocking Chromium-based Edge. I investigated the cause through Datadog traces and rack middleware to a version change in the `browser` gem, implemented the fix, and wrote Capybara feature tests to prevent a regression. The full troubleshooting walkthrough — from symptom detection to root cause to fix — became a [blog post](https://danielabaron.me/blog/ruby-upgrade-broke-ms-edge/).

The Rails upgrade surfaced a different kind of problem: `Rails.application.secrets` being replaced by `Rails.application.credentials` — a migration path that Rails has evolved across four major versions without a clear upgrade guide. I traced the history from Rails 4.1 through 7.2, documented two solution paths, and [published a comprehensive guide](https://danielabaron.me/blog/migrating-from-rails-secrets-to-credentials/) to fill the gap the official docs left open.

Other work includes:

- Security hardening: session timeout via ActionCable, VPN-restricted admin routes
- Overhauled payment failure emails from two generic messages to six plan-specific workflows with accurate data and retention-focused messaging
- Investigated Heroku capacity ahead of a major Black Friday promotion and implemented horizontal scaling to prevent memory issues during peak traffic
- Developer experience improvements: overhauled onboarding documentation, simplified Docker setup, Rubocop enforcement in CI, more reliable seed data and test infrastructure

## Volunteer Work

I have contributed to [Human Essentials](https://github.com/rubyforgood/human-essentials), a Rails application by [Ruby for Good](https://rubyforgood.org/about-us) that helps essentials banks manage and distribute supplies to charitable organizations. Here are my [merged PRs](https://github.com/rubyforgood/human-essentials/pulls?q=is%3Apr+is%3Aclosed+author%3Adanielabar).

The Distribution Confirmation feature is a good example of how engineering problems unfold in practice. The requirement: add a confirmation step with details about a distribution before submitting the form. Seemingly simple. Although this had to be a styled view showing all the items in a distribution, not a simple JavaScript alert. The implementation took three distinct approaches before landing on the right one.

The first approach stored form state in the session — abandoned when it became clear that distributions can have unlimited line items and Rails session cookies have a 4KB limit. The second approach persisted a pending distribution in the database — it worked, but required decoupling tightly coupled business logic in the existing service layer, updating every query in the application to exclude pending records, and creating a cleanup job for abandoned distributions. Too much risk and complexity for what should have been a low-stakes UI change. The third approach intercepted form submission with a Stimulus controller and displayed a client-side modal, with a server-side validation endpoint to pre-check the form before showing the confirmation. This required debugging into Rails ActionPack source to resolve a CSRF token edge case in tests — there are two tokens in play, and the solution required understanding why.

Every iteration including discussions with PM and technical reviewers is documented with links to the PRs, comments, and commits in the [walkthrough notes](https://gist.github.com/danielabar/2f9a96face575973e6cf3b1eb2f963f6).

Other feature work on this project led to blog posts on problems I encountered and solved. One covers a [common Rails pain point with Active Storage](https://danielabaron.me/blog/active_storage_form_errors/): when a form includes a file upload and fails validation, the uploaded file is silently lost on re-render, forcing users to select it again. The post walks through a solution using direct uploads, signed IDs, and a Stimulus controller — with a [companion demo repo](https://github.com/danielabar/expense_tracker). Another covers a [subtle validation issue with nested attributes](https://danielabaron.me/blog/rails-reject-nested-attributes/): when a parent form includes optional child model fields via `accepts_nested_attributes_for`, blank child fields trigger validation errors that roll back the entire transaction. The post explains the `reject_if` option and its variations, also with a [demo repo](https://github.com/danielabar/nested_model_demo).

## Public Projects

The projects below span many years and illustrate different aspects of my work:

**[RRSP/GIS Calculator](https://rrspgiscalculator.site/)** — in active use by financial planners specializing in low-income clients. Background: I heard a policy expert on a podcast discuss a counterintuitive problem: low-income Canadians are routinely advised to save in an RRSP, but for those also eligible for the Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS), RRSP withdrawals in retirement reduce their GIS payment by 50 cents on the dollar. Combined with other taxes, this produces effective marginal tax rates of up to 70% on the lowest-income seniors. Many planners and their clients don't know this.

I reached out to the guest after the podcast with an idea to build a free open-source calculator and visualization tool so people could more easily understand the issue. The collaboration had no structure: no PM, no designer, no prior relationship, no spec. He had deep domain expertise; what he couldn't do was translate it into software requirements or a product vision — that was my problem to solve. I extracted the rules from his case studies, reasoned out the arithmetic, defined what needed to be communicated and how, and iterated on the visualization until the counterintuitive result became immediately legible. The calculator is a React application with full test coverage.

I later collaborated with the same policy expert to develop an [OAS delay calculator](https://danielabar.github.io/oas-delay-calculator-prototype/) — a visualization tool that shows low-income seniors why delaying Old Age Security can cost them tens of thousands of dollars in combined benefits, contradicting the mainstream advice to wait until 70. I wrote a two-part series on building the prototype with an AI assistant — including where it fell short and how engineering judgment filled the gaps — from defining business rules and the initial visualization in [Part 1](https://danielabaron.me/blog/rapid-prototype-chatgpt-oas-breakeven-part1/), to enabling user inputs and refining the interface in [Part 2](https://danielabaron.me/blog/rapid-prototype-chatgpt-oas-breakeven-part2/).

**[Retirement Drawdown Simulator](https://github.com/danielabar/retirement_drawdown_simulator_canada)** — a Ruby CLI that models Canadian retirement drawdown strategies across RRSP/RRIF, TFSA, OAS, CPP, and cash cushion interactions. Running the simulator produced some surprising results: the 4% withdrawal rule is [less reliable than commonly assumed](https://github.com/danielabar/retirement_drawdown_simulator_canada/blob/main/docs/insights/four_percent_rule.md), and keeping a [year's worth of spending in cash rather than invested](https://github.com/danielabar/retirement_drawdown_simulator_canada/blob/main/docs/insights/cash_cushion_vs_invested.md) doesn't significantly improve success rates. The repository includes [architecture documentation](https://github.com/danielabar/retirement_drawdown_simulator_canada/blob/main/docs/architecture.md) and a detailed [explanation of the finance logic](https://github.com/danielabar/retirement_drawdown_simulator_canada/blob/main/docs/how-it-works.md).

**[Hello Visitor](https://github.com/danielabar/hello-visitor)** — a Rails application deployed on Heroku that provides privacy-focused analytics for this blog (no cookies, no tracking). I later added PostgreSQL full-text search to support the blog as well. The [five-part series on how the search was built](https://danielabaron.me/blog/roll-your-own-search-service-for-gatsby-part1/) covers the full stack from motivation through PostgreSQL search concepts, service layer, and React frontend components. Feature tests are written in Cucumber — [near-plain-English behavioral specs](https://github.com/danielabar/hello-visitor/blob/main/features/visit_analysis.feature) that describe what the system does, not just that the code runs.

**[Tidysum](https://github.com/danielabar/tidysum)** — a CLI published to npm that I use to track personal spending and calculate a personal inflation rate. I wrote two posts using this project as the subject: one on [adding a feature with TDD](https://danielabaron.me/blog/tdd-by-example/) and one on [fixing a bug with TDD](https://danielabaron.me/blog/tdd-by-example-bugfix/) — the bug being silent incorrect output caused by missing input validation.

**Others:** [a streaming LLM chatbot in Rails with Turbo Streams and Stimulus](https://github.com/danielabar/ollama_chat), [a web native router built without frameworks](https://github.com/danielabar/web_native_routing) to understand what frameworks abstract away, [a no-framework breathing app](https://danielabar.github.io/just-breathe/) with a blog post that explains the [motivation and how it was built](https://danielabaron.me/blog/building-a-no-frills-meditation-app/), [an interactive ecosystem graph explorer](https://github.com/danielabar/globi-proto) built with a team of four for an InfoViz course and [published on FigShare](https://figshare.com/articles/journal_contribution/GloBI_Explorer_Interactive_Ecosystem_Explorer/1414253?file=2067169) in 2015. The record goes back to 2013: a Java animation frame generator with a shared core library, parallel image processing, and two interfaces built on top: a CLI and a Swing desktop application — built when CSS animations weren't yet a practical option.

**A common thread across these projects:** These were not handed to me as requirements. They started with a problem I noticed or an idea I wanted to explore, and I carried it through the full cycle — analysis, design, implementation, testing, and delivery — on my own initiative. That kind of end-to-end product ownership is hard to demonstrate in a timed setting, but it's visible throughout the work above.

That same curiosity sometimes gets sparked by problems I encounter in other contexts. A [run-length encoding implementation in Ruby](https://danielabaron.me/blog/python-interview-question-in-ruby/) started from a tweet posing a Python interview question — I wanted to see how I'd approach it in another language, with tests, using TDD. A [fair distribution algorithm](https://gist.github.com/danielabar/8cb5f1a8ee926f2bad63cd03e2afffdf) came from a problem I kept thinking about after a conversation — the kind of problem that's hard to solve well in an hour but rewards the time spent working through the constraints carefully. Both are the kind of algorithmic work that benefits from space to think, test, and explain clearly.

## Writing & Learning

Engineering documentation is something I've long advocated for - internal docs that let developers onboard without getting blocked or pulling a teammate off their own work. I've written about this from several angles: [how async-first teams depend on good docs](https://danielabaron.me/blog/working-towards-asynchronous-future/), [what to include and how to make docs engineers actually want to write](https://danielabaron.me/blog/about-those-docs/), and [the documentation practices that make delivery teams effective](https://danielabaron.me/blog/reflections-on-effective-teams/). It turns out that same investment now pays a second dividend: well-structured project documentation is exactly what AI coding assistants need to be effective — a point I explored in [The Machines Finally Made Us Care About Documentation](https://danielabaron.me/blog/machines-made-us-care-about-documentation/).

That same instinct to write things down led to blogging. I started about six years ago to document and share my problem-solving process — the kind of thing I wished had existed when I was working through the same problems. Over [100 posts](https://danielabaron.me/blog/) later, I get outreach from developers who found my posts useful in their own work and learning. Some have been featured in Ruby Weekly, Short Ruby Newsletter, Women on Rails, and Rails Upgrade News, and some make the top page of Google search results. Consistent recognition across different publications and platforms suggests the writing is genuinely useful to people.

The habit of teaching predates the blog. A [presentation on JavaScript unit testing](https://danielabar.github.io/js-unit-test-presentation/) in 2014 — the team was transitioning from server-side Java to full-stack JS, front-end complexity was increasing, and constant regressions made it clear the team needed JS unit testing discipline. An [Aurelia framework lunch-and-learn](https://danielabar.github.io/aurelia-presentation/) in 2016 — the company had built on AngularJS, then Angular 2 was announced as a complete rewrite with no upgrade path, and the team needed to evaluate alternatives. A talk on [engineering effectiveness](https://danielabar.github.io/when/) in 2021 at a company-wide engineering conference — after reading "When: The Scientific Secrets of Perfect Timing," the advice on chronotypes and task scheduling was transformative enough that it was worth formalizing and sharing with the full engineering organization.

I also value continuous learning, and sharing what I'm learning in public helps others too. Repos on [MongoDB performance](https://github.com/danielabar/mongo-performance) and [WebGL](https://github.com/danielabar/coursera-webgl) have picked up stars and forks from developers working through the same material. More of my public course notes are available on the [learning](https://danielabaron.me/learning/) section of my blog.

If you're interested in continuing the conversation, I'd welcome a discussion about what you're building, what challenges your team is facing, where you want to be in a few years, and how I could help you get there.
